Introduction

Subsequent Publications

After its initial publication, the Testimonie of Antiquitie was reprinted several times by other people. The earliest of these I have encountered is in a 1623 book published by William L’isle titled A Saxon Treatise concerning the Old and New Testament [etc.] along side the titular treatise, a translation of Ælfric’s letter to Sigweard.[1] A different, smaller Old English typeface than the one commissioned by Parker is used. This book includes at its front a table of Saxon characters and the same Bible verse, Jeremiah 6:16, that was printed on the Testimonie title page (fig. 2). From this page alone, it is clear the appearance of A Saxon Treatise was heavily influenced by Parker’s book.

Just a year later, in 1624, the Testimonie appears again in Three rare monuments of antiquitie [etc.] printed by Edward Raban for David Melvill with two other texts.[2] In 1638, L’isle’s A Ssaxon Treatise was reprinted under the title Divers ancient Monuments in the Saxon Tongue [etc.] by, according to the title page, E. G. for Francis Eglesfield in London.[3]

The Modern English text of the Testimonie was reprinted under the same title by Leonard Lichfield at Oxford in 1675 (fig. 3).[4] It includes the same preface, translation, epistle, and letter from the bishops, but omits the prayers. Leonard Lichfield (bap. 1637, d. 1686) was the son of another Leonard Lichfield (bap. 1604, d. 1657) who had been official printer to Oxford University. His son, despite claiming the role on the title page of his Testimonie printing, does not seem to have held the position of official printer to the university.[5]

In the eighteenth century, the Modern English text was again printed without its Old English counterpart. A 1736 book with the title A Testimony of Antiquity Concerning the Sacramental Body and Blood [etc.] indicates it “Printed for the Author” but I have found no suggestion of that author’s identity. It includes a dedication to “the Rev. Dr. Waterland”, I presume to be theologian Daniel Waterland,[6] signed “Reverend SIR, With all possible Respect, Your very humble Servant.” This dedication is followed by an introduction of Parker taken from The Life and Acts of Matthew Parker by John Strype, then the usual contents of the Testimonie (sans Old English pages), followed by “Dr. Cave’s Account of Ælfric, and the two Epistles” and several appendices.[7]

The most recent printing of Parker’s Testimonie I will mention is from 1877. Again, the Modern English translation alone was printed, this time edited with marginal and foot notes by Walter Arthur Copinger (figs. 4 and 5).[8]
